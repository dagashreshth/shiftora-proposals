01

The visa platform we built

This is not a pilot and it is not a demo; it is ITL’s industry, region, and workflow, running live in production. This section covers what shipped, the technology inside it, and the architecture as it runs today. If ITL reads one section of this document, it should be this one.

End-to-end visa platform & AI travel orchestration
GCC travel enterprise · web + app

Visa processing was manual and document-heavy, with errors surfacing only at submission, and support teams could not reason across a customer’s own passport and visa history. We designed, built, and launched the entire platform, web and app: a consumer visa product comparable in scope to Atlys, together with one AI travel brain that runs travel operations from fare search and itineraries through to visa follow-up.

What we built
  • The whole platform, complete: discovery and eligibility through application, payment, submission, and tracking, on Next.js web and React Native mobile, live in production 11 weeks after kickoff.
  • Document intelligence: OCR (optical character recognition, the machine reading of documents) with confidence-routed extraction; any field the system is not confident about routes to human review, and no application proceeds until it validates against country rules.
  • The visa engine: eligibility, pricing, and document requirements defined as versioned configuration per route and visa type; rule changes ship as new versions, so any past decision can be reproduced exactly as the rules stood that day.
  • The AI travel brain, one intelligence for everything travel: it hunts the cheapest viable fare across suppliers, builds itineraries, finds the best discount on every booking, tracks every order and refund, drives visa applications and follow-ups, drafts and files dispute refutations, and heals its own broken support conversations. Failed steps are retried, re-planned, or escalated to a person; nothing consequential executes without approval.

What the spine replaces

  • Rules held in people’s heads become configuration: who is eligible, what it costs, which documents apply, and what steps follow are all defined by operations staff, not engineers.
  • Re-keying between systems disappears: one application record flows from intake to decision, and every touch is logged against it.
  • Uncaught errors at submission become validation at entry: an application cannot advance while it breaches the rules of its route.
  • Status enquiries become automatic notification: the traveller is informed at each stage without an agent writing an email.

Why this anatomy fits ITL

  • Rules differ by market. ITL operates across the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, Oman, and beyond; a versioned rules spine holds each market’s requirements without forking the platform.
  • One spine, many fronts. B2C and B2B share one API core, so the same engines can sit behind ITL’s portal, its corporate desks, and its sub-agent network without the front ends changing.
  • Institutional knowledge is preserved, not displaced: what senior visa staff know becomes configuration the whole firm executes consistently, in every office.
  • Scale stops costing linearly. Volume growth loads the system, not the headcount plan.

03

What we see at ITL

Four observations drawn from ITL’s published footprint and from how operations at this scale typically run, beginning with the one that matters most: visas. They are offered as the lens we would bring rather than as settled conclusions; the people running the operation will see further than we can from outside.

Observation 01 · visa operations

The portal captures; people process.

Expert-guided handling from form-filling through submission is a genuine service commitment; at ITL’s volumes, north of a million applications by our research, it is also a cost line, an error surface, and a growth ceiling headcount cannot lift.

Observation 02 · distribution

One platform core could power every front.

Five sub-brands, corporate desks, and a wide sub-agent network serve travel from one operation, and Tripmakers already gives flights and hotels a shared B2B rail. Visas have no equivalent spine yet; a visa core built once would sit behind every front at once.

Observation 03 · service scaling

Service promises currently scale with headcount.

Express visas, overnight rebooking, and round-the-clock GDS queue coverage are sustained by shift work today, and every manual touch erodes a fee-based margin.

Observation 04 · client reporting

Reports are assembled, not generated.

RFP responses and periodic reviews consume analyst-days of manual compilation, and quality is bounded by available time rather than by the data.

Context

On a fee-based margin, every minute of manual handling removed accrues directly to profit, and ITL, with visas at its core, holds an unusually large stock of them.

How to read this. The model has three inputs: monthly application volume, minutes freed per application, and loaded cost per minute. We deliberately do not quote a payback date, because volume sets it: every application processed returns a slice of the build fee, and the return compounds with throughput. At ITL’s visa volumes, which our research puts above a million applications processed, the platform pays for itself very quickly by any reading of the inputs. Every input is ITL’s to correct. The model is also deliberately conservative: it prices staff minutes only. Where a fully manual process lets errors ride until submission, each round trip costs the traveller days; that saved turnaround, and the service reputation it buys, sits on top of the arithmetic.
